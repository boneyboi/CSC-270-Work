106 96. One should part from life as Ulysses parted from Nausicaa--blessing it rather than in love with it.
107 97. What? A great man? I always see merely the play-actor of his own ideal.
108 98. When one trains one's conscience, it kisses one while it bites.
109 99. THE DISAPPOINTED ONE SPEAKS--"I listened for the echo and I heard only praise."
110 100. We all feign to ourselves that we are simpler than we are, we thus relax ourselves away from our fellows.
111 101. A discerning one might easily regard himself at present as the animalization of God.
112 102. Discovering reciprocal love should really disenchant the lover with regard to the beloved. "What! She is modest enough to love even you? Or stupid enough? Or--or---"
113 103. THE DANGER IN HAPPINESS.--"Everything now turns out best for me, I now love every fate:--who would like to be my fate?"
114 104. Not their love of humanity, but the impotence of their love, prevents the Christians of today--burning us.
115 105. The pia fraus is still more repugnant to the taste (the "piety") of the free spirit (the "pious man of knowledge") than the impia fraus. Hence the profound lack of judgment, in comparison with the Church, characteristic of the type "free spirit"--as ITS non-freedom.
116 106. By means of music the very passions enjoy themselves.
117 107. A sign of strong character, when once the resolution has been taken, to shut the ear even to the best counter-arguments. Occasionally, therefore, a will to stupidity.
118 108. There is no such thing as moral phenomena, but only a moral interpretation of phenomena.
119 109. The criminal is often enough not equal to his deed: he extenuates and maligns it.
120 110. The advocates of a criminal are seldom artists enough to turn the beautiful terribleness of the deed to the advantage of the doer.
121 111. Our vanity is most difficult to wound just when our pride has been wounded.
122 112. To him who feels himself preordained to contemplation and not to belief, all believers are too noisy and obtrusive; he guards against them.
123 113. "You want to prepossess him in your favour? Then you must be embarrassed before him."
124 114. The immense expectation with regard to sexual love, and the coyness in this expectation, spoils all the perspectives of women at the outset.
125 115. Where there is neither love nor hatred in the game, woman's play is mediocre.
126 116. The great epochs of our life are at the points when we gain courage to rebaptize our badness as the best in us.
127 117. The will to overcome an emotion, is ultimately only the will of another, or of several other, emotions.
128 118. There is an innocence of admiration: it is possessed by him to whom it has not yet occurred that he himself may be admired some day.
129 119. Our loathing of dirt may be so great as to prevent our cleaning ourselves--"justifying" ourselves.
130 120. Sensuality often forces the growth of love too much, so that its root remains weak, and is easily torn up.
131 121. It is a curious thing that God learned Greek when he wished to turn author--and that he did not learn it better.
132 122. To rejoice on account of praise is in many cases merely politeness of heart--and the very opposite of vanity of spirit.
133 123. Even concubinage has been corrupted--by marriage.
134 124. He who exults at the stake, does not triumph over pain, but because of the fact that he does not feel pain where he expected it. A parable.
135 125. When we have to change an opinion about any one, we charge heavily to his account the inconvenience he thereby causes us.
136 126. A nation is a detour of nature to arrive at six or seven great men.--Yes, and then to get round them.
137 127. In the eyes of all true women science is hostile to the sense of shame. They feel as if one wished to peep under their skin with it--or worse still! under their dress and finery.
138 128. The more abstract the truth you wish to teach, the more must you allure the senses to it.
139 129. The devil has the most extensive perspectives for God; on that account he keeps so far away from him:--the devil, in effect, as the oldest friend of knowledge.
140 130. What a person IS begins to betray itself when his talent decreases,--when he ceases to show what he CAN do. Talent is also an adornment; an adornment is also a concealment.
