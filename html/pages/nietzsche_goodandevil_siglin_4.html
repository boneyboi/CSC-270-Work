Passages 141-175 urn:cts:fuTexts:nietzsche.goodandevil.siglin:141-175
141 131. The sexes deceive themselves about each other: the reason is that in reality they honour and love only themselves (or their own ideal, to express it more agreeably). Thus man wishes woman to be peaceable: but in fact woman is ESSENTIALLY unpeaceable, like the cat, however well she may have assumed the peaceable demeanour.
142 132. One is punished best for one's virtues.
143 133. He who cannot find the way to HIS ideal, lives more frivolously and shamelessly than the man without an ideal.
144 134. From the senses originate all trustworthiness, all good conscience, all evidence of truth.
145 135. Pharisaism is not a deterioration of the good man; a considerable part of it is rather an essential condition of being good.
146 136. The one seeks an accoucheur for his thoughts, the other seeks some one whom he can assist: a good conversation thus originates.
147 137. In intercourse with scholars and artists one readily makes mistakes of opposite kinds: in a remarkable scholar one not infrequently finds a mediocre man; and often, even in a mediocre artist, one finds a very remarkable man.
148 138. We do the same when awake as when dreaming: we only invent and imagine him with whom we have intercourse--and forget it immediately.
149 139. In revenge and in love woman is more barbarous than man.
150 140. ADVICE AS A RIDDLE.--"If the band is not to break, bite it first--secure to make!"
151 141. The belly is the reason why man does not so readily take himself for a God.
152 142. The chastest utterance I ever heard: "Dans le veritable amour c'est l'ame qui enveloppe le corps."
153 143. Our vanity would like what we do best to pass precisely for what is most difficult to us.--Concerning the origin of many systems of morals.
154 144. When a woman has scholarly inclinations there is generally something wrong with her sexual nature. Barrenness itself conduces to a certain virility of taste; man, indeed, if I may say so, is "the barren animal."
155 145. Comparing man and woman generally, one may say that woman would not have the genius for adornment, if she had not the instinct for the SECONDARY role.
156 146. He who fights with monsters should be careful lest he thereby become a monster. And if thou gaze long into an abyss, the abyss will also gaze into thee.
157 147. From old Florentine novels--moreover, from life: Buona femmina e mala femmina vuol bastone.--Sacchetti, Nov. 86.
158 148. To seduce their neighbour to a favourable opinion, and afterwards to believe implicitly in this opinion of their neighbour--who can do this conjuring trick so well as women?
159 149. That which an age considers evil is usually an unseasonable echo of what was formerly considered good--the atavism of an old ideal.
160 150. Around the hero everything becomes a tragedy; around the demigod everything becomes a satyr-play; and around God everything becomes--what? perhaps a "world"?
161 151. It is not enough to possess a talent: one must also have your permission to possess it;--eh, my friends?
162 152. "Where there is the tree of knowledge, there is always Paradise": so say the most ancient and the most modern serpents.
163 153. What is done out of love always takes place beyond good and evil.
164 154. Objection, evasion, joyous distrust, and love of irony are signs of health; everything absolute belongs to pathology.
165 155. The sense of the tragic increases and declines with sensuousness.
166 156. Insanity in individuals is something rare--but in groups, parties, nations, and epochs it is the rule.
167 157. The thought of suicide is a great consolation: by means of it one gets successfully through many a bad night.
168 158. Not only our reason, but also our conscience, truckles to our strongest impulse--the tyrant in us.
169 159. One MUST repay good and ill; but why just to the person who did us good or ill?
170 160. One no longer loves one's knowledge sufficiently after one has communicated it.
171 161. Poets act shamelessly towards their experiences: they exploit them.
172 162. "Our fellow-creature is not our neighbour, but our neighbour's neighbour":--so thinks every nation.
173 163. Love brings to light the noble and hidden qualities of a lover--his rare and exceptional traits: it is thus liable to be deceptive as to his normal character.
174 164. Jesus said to his Jews: "The law was for servants;--love God as I love him, as his Son! What have we Sons of God to do with morals!"
175 165. IN SIGHT OF EVERY PARTY.--A shepherd has always need of a bell-wether--or he has himself to be a wether occasionally.
